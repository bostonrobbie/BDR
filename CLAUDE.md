### Writing Style Rules
- **NO em dashes (—).** Use commas or short hyphens (-) only. Prefer commas.
- **Minimize hyphens.** Hyphens as mid-sentence dashes ("what breaks first - regression or coverage") are an AI writing signature. Replace with commas ("what breaks first, regression or coverage"). Only use hyphens in true compound words where grammatically required (e.g., "self-healing"). Audit every draft: if more than 1 hyphen appears in the message body (excluding the sign-off), rewrite to remove extras.
- Must sound like Rob wrote it personally. Not AI-generated, not templated, not formulaic.
- Warm, conversational, low-pressure, specific, respectful.
- No feature dumping. Focus on a specific solution to something the prospect is already doing.
- Mobile-friendly: 80-99 words for Touch 1 (data-proven sweet spot: 39.0% reply rate). Max 6-8 short lines with spacing. Absolute ceiling 120 words if needed for coherence.
- **Paragraph spacing for readability.** Every message must have clear visual breaks between sections. The opener, context, proof point, and close should each be their own short paragraph separated by a blank line. No paragraph should exceed 3 sentences. This makes the message scannable on mobile, which is where most LinkedIn messages are read.
- **Never "show your work."** Do NOT reference LinkedIn, profiles, resume facts, or years at a company. The research happens in the backend. The message synthesizes it into a sharp insight.
- No "I noticed" or "I saw" or "based on your profile" or resume recaps. (DATA: "I noticed/saw" = -13.4 pp differential, strongest negative signal)
- No generic assertions like "you must be doing manual testing." Ask, don't assert.
- Reading level: simple words, short sentences, no corporate phrasing or buzzwords.
- Exactly TWO question marks in Touch 1 messages (DATA: 2 questions = 34.8% reply rate vs 23.4% for 1 question and 14.3% for 3+).
- **Close must use "what day works" pattern AND tie to the proof point.** (DATA: "What day available" = 40.4% reply rate, best CTA at scale. Open-ended question closes = 14.0%, nearly 3x worse.) The close must reference the specific outcome from the proof point (e.g., "3 fewer weeks," "halving manual testing," "70% less maintenance") and connect it to the prospect's situation, then ask "what day works" for the meeting. See Pre-Draft Step 4 and Close Construction Rules below for detailed guidance.
- **No easy-out lines.** Do not say "no worries," "if not, all good," "feel free to ignore," or any variant. Just ask for the meeting confidently and casually.
- Do not frame anything as benefiting us. No "I want to show you" or "I'd love to share."
- **No generic closes.** The following are BANNED as standalone closes because they work for any message and signal zero personalization: "Worth comparing notes?", "Worth a quick chat?", "Would exploring that be worth your time?", "Thought it would be worth connecting.", "Worth a quick compare?" If the close could be copy-pasted into a different prospect's message without changing a word, it fails.

### Message Structure (C2 Style - Feb 2026 Update)
C2 replaces C1. The goal is the same - one coherent thought from someone who understands their world - but C2 adds guardrails against the "stitched template" problem that crept into C1 batches. Every message must read like a single human idea, not three AI-generated blocks glued together.

**BEFORE writing any message, complete these 3 pre-draft steps:**

#### Pre-Draft Step 1: Single Theme Rule
State the message theme in ONE sentence: "This message is about ______."
- Example: "This message is about flaky Selenium tests blocking a mobile platform refresh."
- Example: "This message is about regression upkeep across three acquired codebases."
- Every sentence in the message must support this theme. After drafting, run a coherence check: delete any sentence that doesn't connect to the theme.

#### Pre-Draft Step 2: QA-Relevant Research Filter
Gather 5-8 research bullets, but ONLY from categories that affect QA outcomes:
- **Release process signal:** frequency, stability focus, migrations, major refreshes
- **Testing stack:** Selenium/Playwright/Cypress, CI tools, device matrix, integration test burden
- **QA org pressure:** hiring for QA/SDET, layoffs, team scaling, compliance deadlines, platform consolidation
- **Integration complexity:** number of integrations, platform permutations, regulated workflows

**AVOID using in messages:** general revenue stats, generic "AI strategy," broad corporate announcements, funding rounds, user counts - UNLESS they directly create testing workload. A $100M funding round is only relevant if it's causing integration sprawl or hiring pressure on the QA team. $4.5B revenue is never relevant.

#### Pre-Draft Step 3: Plain Language Pass
After the first draft, run this cleanup:
- Replace buzzwords: velocity -> speed, bottleneck -> blocker, maintenance sprawl -> test upkeep, bandwidth -> capacity, leverage -> use, scalability -> growth
- Replace filler connector phrases: "That's where teams hit the wall" -> cut it or say what actually happens. "That's the squeeze most teams feel" -> cut it or be specific.
- Keep sentences under 15 words when possible. Use concrete nouns and verbs.
- Read it out loud. If you wouldn't say it in a real conversation, rewrite it.

#### Pre-Draft Step 4: Close Construction
Before writing the close, answer these 3 questions:
1. **What specific outcome did the proof point achieve?** (e.g., "cut regression from 8 to 5 weeks," "halved manual testing time," "cut Selenium upkeep by 70%")
2. **What is the prospect's version of that problem?** (e.g., "OpenEdge release schedule," "keeping coverage ahead of releases at Lucid," "the 2026 platform refresh at Housecall Pro")
3. **How do I connect #1 to #2 and end with 'what day works'?**

The close formula: **[Proof point outcome reframed for their situation] + [what day works for a quick look/chat/compare]**

Vary the close structure across prospects in each batch. Use these patterns and rotate:
- **"If [outcome] would help [their situation], what day works for a quick look at how they did it?"**
- **"What day works to see how [customer] [achieved outcome] while [solving their version of the problem]?"**
- **"If [outcome] before [their event/deadline] sounds useful, what day works for a quick look?"**
- **"What day works to compare how [customer] [achieved outcome] without [their constraint]?"**
- **"If getting [specific number] back in your [their process] would help, what day works for a quick chat?"**

No two prospects in the same batch should have structurally identical closes.

#### Message Elements (5 parts, invisible as structure)

1. **Subject line** (3-6 words) - Specific enough to open, not clickbaity. Reference their domain or a QA-relevant situation. "Quick question" works. So does "Regression after the migration."
2. **Opener** (1-2 lines) - Start with a QA situation the prospect would recognize, then ask a real question. Do NOT start by listing products, company facts, or background. Start with a problem.
   - GOOD: "Are flaky tests slowing your releases or just eating engineering time?"
   - GOOD: "What's harder right now - keeping regression stable during the migration, or keeping coverage from falling behind?"
   - BAD: "With 12+ products across OpenEdge and cloud, how is your team keeping regression manageable?" (this is a resume recap disguised as a question)
   - BAD: "Processing $450B annually through Paymode with PCI/AML requirements..." (this is company trivia, not a QA problem)
3. **Context** (1-2 lines) - One direct sentence explaining why this matters. No filler phrases. Say what actually happens to QA teams in this situation. Be specific, not vague.
   - GOOD: "During platform overhauls, flaky tests usually break faster than the team can fix them."
   - BAD: "That's the bottleneck most mobile teams hit during major overhauls." (vague, formulaic)
4. **Proof point** (0-1 lines) - ONE verified customer example with real numbers, matched to pain. If it doesn't fit naturally into the theme, skip it entirely. Do not force a proof point. Mention Testsigma once here if used. Frame as what the customer achieved.
5. **Close** (1-2 lines) - Must do TWO things: (a) reference the specific outcome from the proof point above, and (b) use a "what day works" direct meeting ask. The close is where the proof point outcome meets the prospect's specific situation, and then asks for the meeting. No generic closes. No easy outs. Must connect the proof point's numbers/outcome to their world.
   - GOOD: "If 3 fewer weeks of regression would help the OpenEdge schedule, what day works for a quick look at how they did it?" (ties "8 to 5 weeks" → "3 fewer weeks" to Irfan's product)
   - GOOD: "What day works to see how Medibuddy cut test upkeep in half while keeping coverage ahead of releases?" (names the customer, echoes the outcome, ties to Katie's problem)
   - GOOD: "If 70% less Selenium maintenance before the refresh sounds useful, what day works for a quick look?" (echoes the stat + her tool + her event)
   - BAD: "Worth comparing notes on how they managed it?" (generic, could go in any message)
   - BAD: "Would exploring that be worth your time?" (generic, no proof point tie-in, no meeting ask)
   - BAD: "What would cutting 3 weeks off regression mean for your schedule?" (ties to proof point but is an open-ended question, not a meeting ask. DATA: open-ended question closes = 14.0% vs "what day works" = 40.4%)

**What the message must NOT sound like:**
- "Hey [Name], I saw you worked at [Company] for [X] years leading [team]. That must mean [assumption]. [Client] saved [number]. Would 15 minutes make sense?"
- "Hello [Name], with [company fact A] and [company fact B] and [company fact C], how is your team keeping up? That's the squeeze most teams feel. [Proof point]. Would exploring that be worth your time?"
- Both formulas are dead. The second one is the "stitched template" pattern - three disconnected facts in the opener, a generic connector line, a proof point, a generic close. Prospects see through it. C2 messages must feel like one idea written by one person who gets it.

**Anti-pattern: "Stitched Template" Detection**
Before sending, check for these signs the message was stitched together:
- Opener contains 2+ company facts that don't connect to each other
- Context sentence uses "That's the [noun] most [type] teams hit/feel/face" (formulaic connector)
- Proof point could be swapped into any message and still make sense (not tied to theme)
- Close is generic ("Would exploring that be worth your time?" works for any message)
- Close doesn't reference the proof point's specific outcome (number, timeframe, or result)
- Close doesn't connect to the prospect's specific situation (their product, their event, their constraint)
- Close uses an open-ended question instead of a "what day works" meeting ask (DATA: 14.0% vs 40.4%)
- Message contains 2+ hyphens used as mid-sentence dashes (AI writing signature)
- Message has no paragraph breaks between proof point and close (wall of text on mobile)
If ANY of these are true, rewrite.

### 3-Touch Sequence (write all touches per prospect)
Every prospect gets a 3-touch sequence across two channels: LinkedIn InMail and Email. No cold calls. All written touches follow the same writing style rules. Rob decides exact timing/spacing manually.

**Day 1 - Touch 1 (LinkedIn InMail):** Full message with all 5 elements (subject, opener, context, proof point, close). 80-120 words.
**Day 5 - Touch 2 (LinkedIn InMail Follow-up):** Shorter, 40-70 words. New angle or new proof point, NOT a rehash of Touch 1. Reference that you reached out before but keep it light ("Circling back quick..."). Add one new piece of value: a different customer story, a specific capability match, or a recent company event. End with a softer but still specific close that ties to the new proof point (e.g., "If cutting regression time in half sounds useful, what day works for a quick look?"). Follow-up closes can be lighter than Touch 1 but must still reference the proof point outcome.
**Day 10 - Touch 3 (Email):** Short email (if email address is available). 60-100 words. Same rules as InMail but can be slightly more direct since email feels less intrusive. Fresh approach with a different proof point than Touches 1 and 2.

**Sequence rules:**
- Each touch must use a DIFFERENT proof point or angle than the previous touch.
- Touch 2 does NOT need the full 5-element structure. It can be looser.
- If a prospect has Buyer Intent, Touch 2 can be more direct ("Noticed your team's been exploring options in this space...").
- Not every prospect will have email addresses. If no email, skip Touch 3 and the sequence ends after Touch 2.
- The sequence ends after Touch 3 (or Touch 2 if no email). No break-up message.

### Research Requirements (THREE sources per prospect)
Every prospect requires research from all three sources below. The goal is to find QA-relevant insights that feed the Single Theme Rule, the proof point match, AND the close construction. Research that doesn't connect to one of those three outputs is wasted effort.

**Source 1: LinkedIn Profile (Sales Navigator)**
- Read headline, about section, role description, responsibilities, recent activity, endorsements, posts.
- Capture and log the Sales Navigator profile URL.
- **What to extract for messaging:**
  - Their specific QA/testing scope (what products, platforms, or systems do they test?)
  - Team size signals (do they mention team growth, hiring, scaling?)
  - Tech stack clues (Selenium, Cypress, Playwright, Appium, specific CI tools mentioned?)
  - Career trajectory (recently promoted? new to this company? came from a company with known testing challenges?)
  - Pain signals in their own words (any posts or comments about testing challenges, tool frustrations, release delays?)
- **What NOT to use in messages:** years at company, education, certifications, job history timeline, endorsement counts. These are "resume recap" signals that kill reply rates (DATA: -13.4 pp).

**Source 2: Apollo Enrichment**
- Before building any outreach, check Apollo for existing contact records to avoid duplicates.
- Use Apollo organization enrichment to pull: employee count, industry, technologies used, funding history, revenue range.
- Use Apollo people enrichment to pull: verified email, phone, current title, department.
- **What to extract for messaging:**
  - Technology stack (Apollo tracks technologies used, which can reveal testing tools, CI/CD platforms, cloud providers)
  - Company size and growth trajectory (funding rounds signal scaling pressure on QA)
  - Industry classification (maps to proof point matching and vertical-specific pain hooks)
  - Contact data for multi-channel sequence (email for Touch 3)
- **Apollo workflow:** Search contacts first (check for duplicates) → Enrich person → Enrich organization → Log all data in prospect card → Use tech stack and industry data to select proof point and construct close.

**Source 3: Company Research (External)**
- From credible external sources: company website, product pages, engineering blog, job postings, press releases, news articles, Glassdoor engineering reviews, GitHub repos, conference talks.
- NOT just the LinkedIn company page. LinkedIn company pages are shallow and everyone uses them.
- **What to extract for messaging (QA-relevant only, per Pre-Draft Step 2):**
  - **Release process signals:** How often do they ship? Are they migrating platforms? Launching new products? Rebuilding a mobile app?
  - **Testing stack evidence:** Job postings mentioning specific tools (Selenium, TOSCA, Katalon, etc.), engineering blog posts about testing practices, open-source test repos.
  - **QA org pressure:** Are they hiring QA/SDET roles? Did they recently lay off QA? Are they consolidating teams post-acquisition?
  - **Integration complexity:** How many integrations, APIs, or platform permutations do they support? Are they in regulated industries requiring compliance testing?
  - **Recent events that create testing workload:** Product launches, acquisitions, platform migrations, new compliance requirements, major feature releases.
- **What NOT to use in messages:** Revenue figures, user counts, funding amounts, generic "AI strategy" announcements, executive quotes, unless they directly create QA workload. (See Pre-Draft Step 2 research filter.)

**Research-to-Message Pipeline:**
The research feeds the message in this order:
1. LinkedIn profile → identifies the person's QA scope and potential pain → feeds the **opener question**
2. Apollo enrichment → confirms tech stack, company size, industry → feeds **proof point selection** and **objection pre-mapping**
3. Company research → identifies specific QA-relevant events/pressures → feeds the **context paragraph** and **theme sentence**
4. All three combined → the proof point outcome + their specific situation → feeds the **close construction** (Pre-Draft Step 4)

Every research bullet logged in the prospect card should be tagged with which message element it feeds (opener, context, proof match, or close). If a research bullet doesn't feed any element, don't log it.

### Reply Tagging
When a prospect replies (positive, negative, or referral), Rob tags WHAT triggered the reply using a dropdown:
- **Opener** - They referenced or reacted to the personalized opener
- **Pain hook** - They engaged with the problem hypothesis ("yeah, that's exactly our issue")

### A/B Testing Within Batches
Split each 25-prospect batch into 2-3 sub-groups to test ONE variable at a time. Label each prospect with their A/B group in the tracker.

**What to A/B test (one per batch, rotate across batches):**
1. **Pain hook** - Group A gets "maintenance/flaky tests" angle, Group B gets "release velocity/speed" angle
2. **Proof point style** - Group A gets named customer (Sanofi, CRED), Group B gets anonymous ("a Fortune 100 company")
3. **Opener style** - Group A gets career-reference openers, Group B gets company-metric openers
4. **Ask intensity** - Group A gets "Would 15 minutes make sense?", Group B gets "Happy to share more if helpful"
5. **Message length** - Group A gets 70-80 words (tight), Group B gets 100-120 words (fuller)

**Rules:**
- Only test ONE variable per batch. Keep everything else constant across groups.
- Split groups evenly by persona type and vertical so results aren't skewed.
- Need 3+ batches testing the same variable to draw conclusions (small sample sizes lie).
- Track the A/B variable and group in the HTML tracker for each prospect.

### Common Pitfalls to Avoid
- **Don't send messages.** Rob will copy/paste manually.
- **Don't use the same customer proof point for every message.** Rotate and match to the prospect's pain.
- **NEVER write "I noticed" or "I saw" in ANY message.** DATA: -13.4 pp differential. Not even 1 per batch.
- **Don't include prospects from the same company more than twice** in a single batch (unless explicitly asked).
- **Don't include VPs/C-level unless there's a strong signal.** DATA: VP = 11.9% reply rate, C-Level = 9.1%. Architects (39.3%) and Managers (26.8%) are far better ROI.
- **Don't use "300% faster" or multiplier claims.** DATA: 16.3% reply rate. Use reduction framing instead ("40% fewer bugs" = 39.2%).
- **Don't use "flaky tests" as a hook.** DATA: 16.8% reply rate (-11.9 pp vs baseline). Use "test maintenance" instead (+9.0 pp).
- **Don't send after 6 PM.** DATA: 12.1% reply rate vs 56.5% at lunch. Nearly 5x worse.
- **Don't send on Mondays if avoidable.** DATA: 22.9% vs Thursday's 42.1%. Save Touch 1 for Thu/Tue/Fri.
- **Don't write generic closes.** DATA: open-ended question closes = 14.0% reply rate. "What day works" = 40.4%. Every close must tie the proof point outcome to the prospect's situation AND use a "what day works" meeting ask. If the close could go in any message, rewrite it.
- **Don't overuse hyphens.** Hyphens as mid-sentence dashes are an AI writing signature. Max 1 per message body. Replace with commas.
- **Don't write wall-of-text paragraphs.** Every message needs 4+ paragraph breaks. Opener, context, proof, and close each get their own line. Max 3 sentences per paragraph.
- **Don't skip Apollo enrichment.** Every prospect needs Apollo data (tech stack, industry, contact info) before message writing. Apollo tech stack data directly informs proof point selection and objection mapping.
- **Don't skip company external research.** LinkedIn company pages are not enough. Check product pages, engineering blogs, job postings, press releases for QA-relevant signals. This research feeds the context paragraph and theme sentence.
- **Do prioritize Architects and QA Manager/Leads.** Best reply rates at meaningful volume.
- **Do flag Buyer Intent signals** from Sales Navigator prominently in the tracker.
- **Do use specific reduction percentages** in proof points ("50% reduction" or "3 days to 80 minutes").
- **Do use "What day works?" as the default CTA.** DATA: 40.4% rate, best-performing CTA at scale. Always tie it to the proof point.
- **Do send at lunch (12-1 PM local time).** DATA: 56.5% reply rate, 2.4x the afternoon rate.
- **Do use all 3 research sources** (LinkedIn, Apollo, company external) for every prospect. Tag each research bullet with which message element it feeds.
- **Do vary close structures** across the batch. Use the 5 close patterns from Pre-Draft Step 4 and rotate.

### Message Quality Score (MQS) - 12-Point System

Every outbound message is scored on 4 dimensions (1-3 each). Maximum = 12.

**Dimension 1: Opener Clarity (1-3)**
- 3 = Specific, insight-driven question about the prospect's situation. No "I noticed," no role-recap, no "reaching out."
- 2 = Company-specific reference but generic frame. May include mild role reference.
- 1 = Opens with "I noticed," "Seeing that you're the [Title]," or "Are you looking for..." Template-visible.

**Dimension 2: CTA Confidence (1-3)**
- 3 = "What day works?" with direct tie to the proof point outcome AND the prospect's specific situation. No easy outs. Close cannot be pasted into another message unchanged.
- 2 = Uses "what day works" but the tie to the proof point is weak or generic (e.g., "what day works for a quick look?" without referencing the outcome).
- 1 = "Would it be unreasonable," "happy to share if helpful," phone ask, open-ended question close, or any generic close ("worth comparing notes?").

**Dimension 3: Personalization Density (1-3)**
- 3 = References something only THIS person would recognize (specific project, career move, company event).
- 2 = References their company specifically but opener could apply to anyone in that role.
- 1 = Only swaps name, title, company. Indistinguishable from a template.

**Dimension 4: Friction (1-3, inverted: 3=low friction)**
- 3 = 80-110 words, one proof point, one CTA, exactly 2 questions, no bullet lists, 4+ paragraph breaks, clean and scannable on mobile. Max 1 hyphen in body.
- 2 = 110-120 words, may have 2 proof points, still readable, proper paragraph spacing.
- 1 = Over 120 words, bullet-point features, multiple CTAs, 3+ questions, wall-of-text, 2+ hyphens as dashes.

**Interpretation:**
- 10-12 = Ready to send
- 7-9 = Acceptable but should be improved
- <7 = MUST be rewritten before sending

### QA Gate (MANDATORY before any batch is presented)

1. **Hard Constraint scan**: Check every message against all 10 Hard Constraints. Any violation = auto-rewrite.
2. **Score computation**: Compute MQS for every message. Show breakdown by dimension.
3. **Threshold enforcement**: Only messages scoring >=9/12 may be presented.
4. **Word count check**: Touch 1 must be 80-120 words (sweet spot 95-110). Follow-ups can be 40-70 words.
5. **Question count check**: Exactly 2 question marks for Touch 1.
6. **Structural dedup**: No two messages in the batch may be structurally identical.
7. **Evidence check**: Every personalization claim must have a research source from one of the 3 required sources (LinkedIn, Apollo, company research).
8. **Angle rotation check**: No prospect may receive the same angle in more than one touch.
9. **Phrase toxicity scan**: Flag any message containing toxic phrases (see Phrase-Level Intelligence).
10. **CTA validation**: Close MUST contain "what day works" pattern (DATA: 40.4% reply rate). Close MUST reference the specific outcome from the proof point above it. Close MUST connect that outcome to the prospect's specific situation. If the close could be pasted into a different prospect's message unchanged, it fails. No generic closes ("Worth comparing notes?", "Worth a quick chat?").
11. **Hyphen audit**: Count hyphens in message body (excluding sign-off). Max 1 hyphen allowed, and only for true compound words. Mid-sentence dashes ("what breaks first - regression or coverage") must be replaced with commas. Any message with 2+ hyphens = auto-rewrite.
12. **Paragraph spacing check**: Message must have at least 4 paragraph breaks (\n\n). Opener, context, proof point, and close must each be visually separate paragraphs. No paragraph exceeds 3 sentences.
13. **Close structure dedup**: No two closes in the same batch may use the same sentence structure. Rotate across the 5 close patterns defined in Pre-Draft Step 4.
14. **Research depth check**: Every prospect must have research from all 3 sources (LinkedIn, Apollo, company external). If any source is missing, research is incomplete and the prospect cannot be included in the batch.

| Rule | Rationale |
|------|-----------|
| No browser extensions for LinkedIn automation | 60% higher detection risk; 23% ban rate within 90 days |
| Rob must manually click Send for every message | Automated sending violates LinkedIn ToS |
| No scraping or bulk data extraction from LinkedIn | Violates LinkedIn User Agreement Section 8.2 |
| No VPN or IP spoofing during send sessions | Triggers location-anomaly detection |
| Stay within InMail monthly quota (check credits before each session) | Exceeding quota triggers account review |
| No identical messages to multiple prospects | Template detection flags spam behavior |
| Minimum 3-day gap between follow-up messages to same prospect | Rapid follow-ups trigger harassment flags |

### Pacing Limits (Per Session)
| Activity | Safe Daily Max | Safe Weekly Max | Danger Zone |
|----------|---------------|----------------|-------------|
| InMails sent | 8 | 20 | >15/day or >25/week |
| Profile views | 100 | 500 | >150/day |
| Connection requests | 15 | 80 | >25/day or >100/week |
| Total messages (all types) | 15 | 60 | >20/day |
| Searches | 50 | 250 | >100/day |

### Session Pacing Rules
- Maximum 25 InMails per batch send session (our standard batch size)
- Space sends by at least 2-3 minutes each (never rapid-fire)
- Take a 10-minute break every 10 sends
- Send during business hours only (9 AM - 5 PM local, ideally 12-1 PM)
- Never send on back-to-back days at identical times (varies timing)
- If any "unusual activity" warning appears: STOP immediately, pause 72 hours, reduce volume by 50%

### Account Health Monitoring
Track these metrics in every cycle log:
- InMail credits remaining (should decrease predictably)
- Any LinkedIn warnings or restrictions
- Connection request acceptance rate (if sending any)
- Profile view count (check in Sales Navigator settings)
- Reply rate trend (declining reply rate may signal deliverability issues)

### Recovery Protocol (If Restricted)
1. **Tier 1 (feature restriction, 1-24 hours):** Stop all activity. Wait 24 hours. Resume at 50% volume.
2. **Tier 2 (account lock, 3-14 days):** Submit ID verification. Pause all outreach 7 days after recovery. Resume at 25% volume, gradually increase.
3. **Tier 3 (permanent ban):** Escalate to Rob immediately. This is unlikely with our workflow but must be documented.

## Warm / Inbound Lead SOP

**Purpose:** When a prospect replies positively, gets referred, or comes inbound (website demo request, event lead, Buyer Intent signal), this SOP defines the response workflow. Warm leads get faster, more direct treatment than cold outreach.

### Trigger Events
| Source | Signal | Response Time Target |
|--------|--------|---------------------|
| InMail reply (positive) | "Interested," "tell me more," "good timing" | Same day, within 2 hours |
| InMail reply (referral) | "Talk to [name]" | Same day to referred person |
| Website demo request | Marketing passes lead | Within 4 hours |
| Buyer Intent (Sales Nav) | Account shows research activity | Next business day (new sequence) |
| Event/webinar attendee | Marketing list | Within 48 hours |
| Re-engagement trigger | Job posting, funding, leadership change | Within 1 week |

### Response Framework (Warm Leads)
1. **Acknowledge fast.** Mirror their language. If they said "interested in the self-healing piece," your reply should reference self-healing specifically.
2. **One proof point, matched to their stated interest.** Don't dump all proof points. Pick the ONE that matches what they asked about.
3. **Bridge to meeting.** "What day works this week for a quick look?" Keep the momentum.
4. **Prep the meeting card.** As soon as status = Meeting Booked, auto-generate the Prep Card (see Meeting Booked Handoff in main SOP).

### Response Framework (Referrals)
1. **Thank the referrer** in a short reply (1-2 lines, genuine).
2. **Research the referred person** using the full 3-source pipeline (LinkedIn, Apollo, Company).
3. **Draft outreach to the referred person** mentioning the referrer by name: "[Referrer name] suggested I reach out..."
4. **Use a softer CTA** since the referral creates implicit trust: "Would a quick call make sense to see if it's relevant?"

### Follow-Up Tracking (Gmail + Calendar)
- When a warm lead replies, create a **Google Calendar reminder** 2 business days out titled "Follow up: [Name] @ [Company]" with notes on what they said and next action.
- If no response to the follow-up within 3 business days, send a second nudge (shorter, lighter).
- After 2 unanswered follow-ups on a warm lead, move to "Dormant - Was Warm" status. Re-engage only on new trigger event.
- Track all warm lead interactions in the batch HTML tracker with "Replied" or "Meeting Booked" status and the appropriate reply tag.

### Gmail Integration for Follow-Up
- Use Gmail search to check for any existing email threads with the prospect before sending.
- Draft follow-up emails in Gmail (Claude drafts, Rob sends).
- Calendar reminders serve as the follow-up tickler system since we're not using Salesforce tasks.
