Testsigma BDR Email Sequence SOP

Data-driven email playbook for Apollo sequences. Built from analysis of 1,326 conversations, 381 replies, 4,786 messages, and Sam McKenna's SMYKM methodology. Updated March 2026.

Why This SOP Exists

Cold email for test automation has a low baseline reply rate (~1-6%). But message structure, word count, CTA choice, and stat framing create massive variance in outcomes. The rules in this SOP are derived from real performance data, not theory. When data conflicts with intuition, the data wins.

Email Writing Rules

Hard Constraints (NEVER violate, auto-reject if broken)

  • HC1: No "I noticed" / "I saw" / "I see" phrasing. -13.4 pp Strongest negative signal.
  • HC2: No leading with AI/ML/self-healing as the headline. -9.2 pp Features repel.
  • HC3: No emails over 100 words (Touch 1). Follow-ups max 80 words. 75-99 words = 39.0%
  • HC4: No evening sends (after 6 PM). 12.1% vs 56.5% at lunch
  • HC5: No bullet-point or numbered-list feature dumps. -2.2 pp
  • HC6: No "would it be unreasonable" as CTA. 12.8% reply rate
  • HC7: No phone-number asks. 5.0% reply rate Worst CTA.
  • HC8: No "reaching out" / "wanted to connect." -2.4 pp
  • HC9: No "I figure" / "would you like to share" / "enough about me." 9.8% to 18.2% rates
  • HC10: No 3+ question marks per email. 14.3% Optimal = exactly 2 questions.

Strong Preferences (optimize for)

  • SP1: 60-100 words for email (sweet spot: 70-90). 39.0% at 75-99
  • SP2: Schedule sends between 12-1 PM prospect's local time. 56.5%
  • SP3: Send on Thursdays or Tuesdays. Avoid Mondays. Thu: 42.1% Mon: 22.9%
  • SP4: Exactly 2 question marks per email. 34.8%
  • SP5: "What day works" CTA tied to the proof point outcome. 40.4%
  • SP6: Include at least one stat with a specific number. 32.8%
  • SP7: Use reduction framing, never multiplier framing. "40% fewer" = 39.2% "300% faster" = 16.3%
  • SP8: Minimum 3 emails in a sequence. 31.1% of replies come after 3+ touches.
  • SP9: Target Architects and QA Manager/Leads first. Best reply rate personas.
  • SP10: Vary the angle and proof point across every touch. Same angle = diminishing returns.

Style Rules

  • No em dashes (—). Use commas. Hyphens only for true compound words (e.g., "self-healing"). Max 1 hyphen per email body.
  • No resume recaps. Never reference LinkedIn, profiles, years at company, or "based on your profile."
  • No feature dumping. Focus on one specific pain and one proof point per email.
  • Mobile-friendly. Short paragraphs (max 2-3 sentences each). Blank line between every paragraph.
  • Simple language. Short sentences, no corporate buzzwords. Read it out loud: if you wouldn't say it in a real conversation, rewrite.
  • No easy-out lines. Never say "no worries," "if not, all good," "feel free to ignore."
  • No generic closes. "Worth comparing notes?" and "Worth a quick chat?" are BANNED. Close must tie proof point to their situation.
  • Don't "show your work." Research happens in the backend. The email synthesizes it into a sharp insight, no citations.

Email-Specific Hard Constraints

  • EC1: Email Touch 1 max 100 words (standard) or 120 words (SMYKM). Follow-ups: see EC2.
  • EC2: Follow-up emails max 70 words. Ultra-short wins (<50w = 72.1%).
  • EC3: Subject lines: 5-6 words standard, up to 8 words for SMYKM personal. Problem-framed. No clickbait.
  • EC4: 1-2 question marks per email. Can be more direct than InMail.
  • EC5: No "circling back" or "following up on my last email." Template-visible, triggers spam filters.
  • EC6: SMYKM subject lines must reference a UNIQUE professional detail, not just title or company name. "QA Manager at Acme" = FAIL. "[Name]'s regression automation at Acme" = PASS.
  • EC7: SMYKM opener must be HC1-compliant. "We have yet to be properly introduced" = PASS. "I noticed you're leading QA" = FAIL. HC1: -13.4 pp still applies

SMYKM Style Rules

  • Subject line uniqueness test: If the subject line could apply to anyone with the same title at the same company, it fails. Must reference something SPECIFIC to this person's professional scope.
  • Challenge-narrative embedding: Don't separate context and proof point into distinct paragraphs. Weave the proof point INTO the challenge description so it reads as one continuous thought.
  • Warm intro, not cold pitch: "We have yet to be properly introduced" signals respect and professionalism. It's the opposite of "I'm reaching out because..." (HC8 violation).
  • Research depth requirement: SMYKM requires deeper research than standard. If you can't find a unique professional detail from LinkedIn/Apollo/company research, use Structure A instead.
  • A/B test guidance: Split 50/50 within a batch. Same personas and verticals in each group. Test for 3+ batches before drawing conclusions.

Email Structure: Two Options

Every email uses one of two structures. Both should read like one coherent idea from someone who understands the prospect's world. Choose based on research depth available. A/B test across batches to find what works best for your vertical and persona mix.

Structure A: Standard (Default)

Problem opener → Context → Proof point → "What day works" close

Use when: Limited personal research available. Any prospect.

Word count: 60-100 words

Structure B: SMYKM (A/B Test Option)

Personal subject → Warm intro → Challenge-narrative with embedded proof → "What day works" close

Use when: Research yields a unique professional detail. Email channel only.

Word count: 80-110 words

Source: Sam McKenna (43% open, 20% reply vs 6% / 0.9% industry avg)

Structure A: Standard Elements (4 Parts)

Element 1: Subject Line (5-6 words)

Problem-framed, specific to their domain. Not clickbaity. Must reference their world.

Good: "Regression after the platform refresh" / "Test maintenance eating your sprint" / "Coverage gap before the release"

Bad: "Quick question" (too vague for email) / "AI-powered testing solution" (feature-led) / "Touching base" (zero value)

Element 2: Opener (1-2 lines)

Start with a QA situation the prospect would recognize, then ask a real question. Do NOT start with who you are or what Testsigma does.

Good: "What's harder right now, keeping regression stable during the migration, or keeping coverage from falling behind?"

Bad: "Hi, I'm [Name] from Testsigma and we help QA teams..." (pitch-first = delete)

Element 3: Context + Proof Point (2-3 lines)

One sentence on why this matters. One proof point with a specific number matched to their pain. Mention Testsigma once here. Frame as what the customer achieved, not what Testsigma does.

Good: "During platform overhauls, test maintenance usually spikes faster than the team can keep up. Hansard cut their regression cycle from 8 weeks to 5 after switching to Testsigma's auto-healing."

Element 4: Close (1-2 lines)

Must do TWO things: (a) reference the specific outcome from the proof point, (b) use a "what day works" meeting ask tied to their situation.

Good: "If 3 fewer weeks of regression would help the platform refresh timeline, what day works for a quick look at how they did it?"

Bad: "Would a quick call make sense?" (generic, no proof point tie-in)

Structure B: SMYKM Elements (4 Parts)

Sam McKenna's "Show Me You Know Me" — A/B test option for email channel only. NOT for InMail.

SMYKM Element 1: Personal Subject Line (up to 8 words)

Hyper-personal. Must reference something UNIQUE about the prospect professionally + their company name. The subject line alone should make them think "this person actually knows who I am."

Good: "Namita's QA coverage expansion at OverDrive" / "The Libby migration testing, Namita" / "Marcus's Selenium stack at Shopify"

Bad: "QA Manager at OverDrive" (title-only, not unique) / "Hi Namita" (no professional detail) / "OverDrive testing" (company-only, no personal element)

SMYKM Element 2: Warm Opener (1-2 lines)

Introduce yourself warmly without "I noticed" or "I saw." HC1-compliant. Two options:

Option A (Intro): "We have yet to be properly introduced, but I'm Rob with Testsigma."

Option B (Direct challenge): Jump straight to the challenge observation: "Scaling QA across three consumer apps while migrating off a legacy platform is a tough ask."

SMYKM Element 3: Challenge-Narrative Value Prop (2-3 lines)

Embed the proof point inside a challenge description. Frame as: "The challenge [type of team] hits is [problem]. [Customer] was dealing with the same thing and [outcome]." This is different from standard structure where context and proof are separate paragraphs.

Good: "The challenge QA teams hit during platform overhauls is that test maintenance spikes faster than the team can keep up. Hansard was in the same spot, running 8 weeks of regression, and cut it to 5 after moving to Testsigma's auto-healing."

SMYKM Element 4: Close (same as Standard)

"What day works" CTA tied to the proof point outcome + prospect's situation. Same rules as Structure A. Sam McKenna uses a softer close ("Do you have time over the next week or two?") but our data shows "what day works" outperforms at 40.4% vs 14.0% for open-ended asks.

Pre-Draft Checklist (complete BEFORE writing)

  • Single Theme: State the email's theme in one sentence. "This email is about ______." Every sentence must support this theme.
  • Research Filter: Only use QA-relevant research (release process, testing stack, QA org pressure, integration complexity). NOT revenue, funding, or generic company facts.
  • Proof Point Match: Select one proof point matched to their pain and vertical. Must include a specific number.
  • Close Construction: Answer: (1) What outcome did the proof point achieve? (2) What's the prospect's version of that problem? (3) How do I connect them with "what day works"?
  • Plain Language Pass: Replace all buzzwords. Read it out loud. If it sounds like AI wrote it, rewrite.

Close Construction Formula

Rotate these 5 patterns across prospects. No two prospects in the same batch get the same close structure.

#PatternExample
1"If [outcome] would help [their situation], what day works for a quick look at how they did it?""If 3 fewer weeks of regression would help the OpenEdge schedule, what day works for a quick look at how they did it?"
2"What day works to see how [customer] [achieved outcome] while [solving their version]?""What day works to see how Medibuddy cut test upkeep in half while keeping coverage ahead of releases?"
3"If [outcome] before [their event/deadline] sounds useful, what day works for a quick look?""If 70% less Selenium maintenance before the refresh sounds useful, what day works for a quick look?"
4"What day works to compare how [customer] [achieved outcome] without [their constraint]?""What day works to compare how Sanofi cut regression from 3 days to 80 minutes without adding headcount?"
5"If getting [specific number] back in your [their process] would help, what day works?""If getting 50% of your manual testing time back would help during the scale-up, what day works?"

Anti-Pattern Detection ("Stitched Template" Check)

Before sending, check for these signs the email was stitched together. If ANY are true, rewrite.

  • Opener contains 2+ company facts that don't connect to each other
  • Context uses "That's the [noun] most [type] teams hit/feel/face" (formulaic connector)
  • Proof point could be swapped into any email (not tied to theme)
  • Close is generic ("Worth comparing notes?" works for any email)
  • Close doesn't reference the proof point's specific outcome
  • Close doesn't connect to the prospect's specific situation
  • Close uses an open-ended question instead of "what day works" 14.0% vs 40.4%
  • Email contains 2+ hyphens used as mid-sentence dashes (AI signature)
  • Email has no paragraph breaks between sections (wall of text on mobile)

Email Sequence Cadence (3-5 Touches)

Recommended Apollo Sequence: 5-Touch, 21-Day

Touch 1 — Day 1 (Tuesday or Thursday, 12-1 PM local)

Full email. Use Standard templates (70-90 words) OR SMYKM templates (80-110 words) based on research depth. Pain-hook opener + proof point + "what day works" close. A/B test SMYKM vs Standard across batches.

Touch 2 — Day 5 (reply thread, "Re:" subject)

Follow-up. NEW angle, different proof point. 40-70 words. Lighter but still specific close.

Touch 3 — Day 10 (fresh subject line, break pattern)

Completely fresh angle. Different proof point. New subject line (not "Re:"). 50-70 words.

Touch 4 — Day 15 (optional, value-add)

Share something useful: relevant case study, industry stat, or competitive insight. 40-60 words. Soft close.

Touch 5 — Day 21 (final touch, direct)

Short and direct. "Wanted to give this one more try." One line of value. "What day works" close. 30-50 words. No breakup email. Just ask one more time.

Cadence Rules

  • Every touch uses a DIFFERENT proof point and angle. Repeating = diminishing returns.
  • Send at 12-1 PM prospect's local time. Schedule in Apollo accordingly. 56.5% reply rate
  • Preferred send days: Thursday, Tuesday, Friday. Avoid Monday for Touch 1. Mon: 22.9%
  • Never send back-to-back days at identical times. Vary by 1-2 hours.
  • Touch 1 and 2 use "Re:" thread. Touch 3+ use fresh subjects to break inbox pattern.
  • Touch 4 is optional. Use for high-priority prospects. Skip for lower-priority to save volume.
  • No breakup emails. "Should I close the file?" has near-zero conversion and wastes the final touch.
  • No "just checking in" follow-ups. Every touch must add new value or a new angle.

QA Checklist (Run Before Every Send)

Every email must pass ALL checks before it enters an Apollo sequence. Any failure = rewrite.

  • HC Scan: Check against all 10 Hard Constraints. Any violation = auto-reject.
  • Word Count: Touch 1 = 60-100 words. Follow-ups = 40-70 words. Count excludes greeting and sign-off.
  • Question Count: Exactly 2 question marks for Touch 1. 1-2 for follow-ups.
  • CTA Validation: Contains "what day works" (or close variant). References proof point outcome. Ties to prospect's situation. NOT generic.
  • Proof Point Present: At least one customer story or stat with a specific number.
  • Reduction Framing: Stats use "cut by X%" or "X fewer," never "X times faster" or "X00% improvement."
  • Subject Line: 5-6 words. Problem-framed. No clickbait.
  • Paragraph Spacing: Minimum 3 paragraph breaks. No paragraph over 3 sentences. Scannable on mobile.
  • Hyphen Audit: Max 1 hyphen in body (compound words only). No mid-sentence dashes.
  • Toxic Phrase Scan: No "I noticed," "I saw," "flaky tests," "I figure," "enough about me," etc.
  • Angle Rotation: Each touch in the sequence uses a DIFFERENT proof point and angle.
  • Close Dedup: No two emails in the same batch share the same close structure.
  • Personalization Check: At least one element is specific to THIS prospect (not just name/title/company swap).
  • Plain Language: No buzzwords (velocity, leverage, scalability, bandwidth). Simple words only.
  • SMYKM Validation (if using Structure B): Subject line references a UNIQUE professional detail (EC6). Opener is HC1-compliant, no "I noticed/saw" (EC7). Challenge-narrative embeds proof point naturally. Word count 80-120 max.

Stat Framing Rules

  • Use reduction framing: "cut by 50%", "3 fewer weeks", "halved their manual testing." 39.2%
  • Never use multiplier framing: "3X faster", "300% improvement." 16.3%
  • Use specific numbers: "from 8 weeks to 5 weeks" beats "significantly reduced." 32.8% with any stat
  • Frame from the customer's perspective: "Hansard cut regression from 8 to 5 weeks" not "Testsigma reduced Hansard's regression by 37.5%."
