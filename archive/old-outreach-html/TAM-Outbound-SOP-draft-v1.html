Who We Target — Personas & Companies

Company Universe

TAM accounts only. Every account must be in one of two lists before any outreach begins:

  • Factor Accounts (38): Listed in memory/target-accounts.md — enterprise accounts with Nektar intent signals (demo, signup, G2, web sessions)
  • TAM Accounts (312): Listed in tam-accounts-mar26.csv — full enterprise TAM list

Enterprise only. No SMB. No commercial. Minimum ~500 employees as a general rule (use judgment — a well-funded 200-person SaaS is fine; a 50-person startup is not).

Target Personas — Maximum Volume, Minimum Wasted Effort

Goal: reach every person who can evaluate a tool, recommend it internally, or approve a purchase. Cast the widest net possible, but only include titles with actual management authority — no individual contributors, no end users.

The DM Authority Rule

Include anyone who manages a team, owns a QA/testing scope, or can influence/approve tool purchases. Exclude anyone who is purely an individual contributor — they can't buy, can't champion, and can't get you a meeting worth having. When uncertain about a title, ask: "Does this person have direct reports or budget influence?" If yes, include. If purely IC, skip.

Volume vs. Quality Balance

More contacts per account = more opportunities, NOT more clutter. Each message is tailored enough that reaching 3 people at Chase is fine. Use the SOP research protocol for each contact individually. Never copy-paste one message to multiple contacts at the same company.

For mid-size enterprise (<2,000 employees): QA Manager/Director is the primary target. For large enterprise (2,000–20,000 employees): Director or above. For mega-enterprise (20,000+ employees — Google, YouTube, Fidelity, Chase): VP-level is required. At this scale, Directors are likely too many layers from budget authority. Go as high as the available verified email will let you go.

Drafting Messages

Step 1 — T1 Email

✅ Confirmed — T1 is Email Only (Old InMail Drafts Deprecated)

Previous Wave 1 InMail drafts (Cboe, Fidelity, Chase, Commvault from wave1-prospecting-plan-mar9.html) are deprecated and deleted. We start fresh with email T1 for all accounts. No InMail path for Step 1 in this sequence. All prospecting begins from zero — no recycled drafts.

Subject line: Use SMYKM (specific to the person and company). Options:

  • "[Name]'s QA coverage at [Company]" — person-first, direct
  • "Testing at [Company's specific platform]" — platform-specific
  • "[Specific pain] at [Company]" — e.g., "Regression coverage at Fidelity"
  • Banned: "Quick question", "Following up", generic pain frames like "Regression eating your time?"

Opening line — HC1-Compliant Intro (required for enterprise contacts):

"We have yet to be properly introduced, but I'm Rob with Testsigma."

This is not optional for Director+ titles at enterprise accounts. Skipping it reads as mass-produced. Immediately follow with a QA-relevant challenge observation tied to their specific role or company — do NOT comment on their profile, LinkedIn activity, or career history.

Body (2-3 sentences): Challenge-narrative + proof point.

Pattern: "[What their type of org faces with QA at their scale/vertical]. [Customer] cut/reached/reduced [specific outcome] using Testsigma's [specific capability]. [Their version of that outcome] — what day works for a quick look at how they did it?"

Word count: 75-100 words. Absolute max 120. Enterprise inboxes = time-scarce. Shorter wins.

Question marks: Exactly 2 in T1 (data: 34.8% reply rate vs. 23.4% for 1 question mark).

Close: Always "what day works" tied to the proof point outcome (40.4% reply rate vs. 14.0% for open-ended).

Step 2 — T2 Email (Day 5)

✅ Unified T2 Formula — Email-First (Redesigned Mar 10)

Designed from scratch for email-first sequences. No LinkedIn callback. Builds directly on T1 with a different angle and new proof point.

Unified T2 Formula (4 parts, 50–70 words):

  1. Bridge (1 sentence): Reference T1 without "following up" or "circling back." Use: "One more angle from my last note." or "Different lens on the same problem." or "Thought this was worth adding."
  2. New trigger or observation (1 sentence): A different angle on their challenge — shift theme entirely. If T1 used maintenance/flakiness, T2 uses creation speed or coverage gaps (or vice versa).
  3. New proof point (1–2 sentences): Different customer story from T1. Tie-back to the new angle.
  4. Engagement question CTA (1 sentence): NOT "what day works" — ask a qualifying question that gets them to confirm the pain. Examples: "Is test creation speed or maintenance more of the pain point right now?" or "Are you mostly running Selenium-based, or something newer?" This lowers the commitment bar and leads to a natural reply.
EXAMPLE T2 (following a T1 that used self-healing / maintenance angle): Subject: Re: [T1 subject] (or: Test creation speed at [Company]) One more angle on the same topic. Beyond maintenance, the other thing teams at your scale tell us is that writing tests fast enough to keep pace with releases is the real bottleneck. One team built 2,500 tests while cutting maintenance 50% — but the speed of initial test creation was actually the bigger unlock for them. Is test creation time or flakiness more of the pain point for your team right now? Rob

T2 rules regardless of formula:

  • New angle — not a repeat of T1's theme
  • New proof point — never the same customer story as T1
  • 40-70 words (even shorter than T1 for the follow-up)
  • CTA = engagement question, NOT "what day works" (save the meeting ask for after engagement)
  • No "following up" / "circling back" language
  • No em dashes

Last touch. Human tone, low-pressure, leaves the door open.
